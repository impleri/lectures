\section{Dogmatic Changes}
\begin{frame}{Religious Fundamentalism in America}
	\begin{itemize}
		\item<+->Multiple influences
			\only<1>{
				\begin{enumerate}
					\item Insularity of religious academ{\'e}
					\item Displacement of theology from academic and social scene (Enlightenment and Industrial Revolution)
					\item Darwin's theory of evolution
					\item Influx of Catholic immigrants (esp. Irish and Italian)
				\end{enumerate}
			}
		\item<+->Some of the popular American Protestant groups joined together as a reaction to these changes 
		\item<+->Met annually to discuss what would be the central tenets for Christians according to them
		\item<+->\emph{The Fundamentals} [1910s]: series of essays eventually collected into a single book. Became the defining feature of their movement
		\item<+->1922. ``Fundamentalism'' in common usage to describe this group
		\item<+->Defending Christian belief in America from the erosion of faith caused by newer ``innovations'' in thought
		\item<+->1927. Scopes ``monkey'' trial in Dayton, Tennessee
		\item<+->Political (and cultural) effects of fundamentalism
			\begin{enumerate}
				\item Prohibition (ratified in 1920)
				\item Creationism as a \emph{scientific} concept
				\item Divide between fundamentalists and ``mainline'' Christianity
				\item Distrust of contemporary science
				\item Resurgence in political conservatism values and Republican presidents moving further into political right
			\end{enumerate}
	\end{itemize}
\end{frame}
